Death Valley, California, United States
Sunday, May 29, 2016

Death Valley is the largest National Park in the USA after Alaska and covers three million acres of designated Wilderness. Leaving Las Vegas behind was easy and again we had a stark contrast when entering Death Valley. We are staying on an oasis, Ranch at Furnace Creek right in the middle of the desert. It's quite bizarre to enter the ranch as you immediately see the lush grass of the golf course.

The highest temperature ever recorded here was 57/134 degrees! Now that's hot. Today we saw 42/107 degrees and that was quite warm enough. It obviously feels extremely hot and dry and as the notices say you need to drink loads. Daytime temperatures exceed 120°F and nights remain hot with overnight lows in the 90s frequently.

The landscape is wild and unforgiving and the country includes low valley floors crusted with barren salt flats, rugged mountains rising as much as 11,000 feet, deep and winding canyons, rolling sand dunes, and spring-fed oases.

We drove to the Badwater Basin which is the lowest point below sea level in USA, -282 ft. It's a massive salt water mostly dry lake which floods with Spring meltwater and in flash floods. On the return drive we detoured via the one way Artists loop to see the multicoloured rock formations. Brings back my A level geography!
