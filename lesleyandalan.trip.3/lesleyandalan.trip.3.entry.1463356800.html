Santa Fe, New Mexico, United States
Monday, May 16, 2016

Leaving our stop over in Lubbock behind the countryside started to open up becoming wide flat fields of deep red ploughed soil. Long outdoor stalls of maybe 50 cows on ranches were seen whilst the loose cows in fields were only in groups of 10-15. Grain and feed silos were frequent although they looked like they had seen better days. The single track railway ran beside the road for miles and we counted trains loaded with over 100 double decker containers pulled and pushed by four engines. They certainly let the motorist know when they are approaching where the road and train cross with their horns.

Pea and bean business, livestock selling places as well rock and stone quarries were often seen. We were surprised to see an advertisement for a cotton seller in these parts. The massive bill board advertising everything from food, to cars to Jesus are much less frequent compared to Louisiana where they continuously spoil the countryside.

Passing from Texas into New Mexico we gained an hour but lost the higher speed limits on the roads. The maximum is now 65 on most of the roads. We stopped at a roadside restaurant for a coffee and had an interesting conversation with the local sheriff who entered at the same time as we did. He looked like he was still at school but had apparently been a sheriff for three years and was proudly wearing his gun, stun gun, two pairs of handcuffs and numerous other implements to be able to keep the peace. Probably much more then those guarding David get eh H! At least he wasn't in his cowboy hat like the locals.

We passed Billy the Kids resting place and later joined Route 66 at Santa Rosa, don't worry we're not arriving for two weeks, we continue on the last leg of the journey. This Santa Rosa is known for its numerous natural springs that are anomalies in the surrounding desert climate, something California Santa Rosa would have been grateful for last year with their drought. You do have your own well of course though!

The dry, arid rocky land dotted with bushes and cacti proved fascinating for us but we quickly started to climb towards our next destination of Santa Fe situated at the foot of the Sangre de Cristo mountain range and could see the snow ahead.

Santa Fe sits on a plateau in the Rocky Mountains at an altitude of 7000 ft above sea level and is the second oldest city and oldest state capital in the US. Founded in 1610 by Spanish missionaries it is also a ski centre. The area is home to New Mexico's indigenous people, the Pueblo Indians. The community is diverse and includes Native Americans, Spenish, Mexicans and Yankees (Unions).

Just north of here is where the first Atomic bomb was created by the Manhattan Project during WW2.

Our B and B is in the traditional Adobe architectural style, certainly unexpected and different. The property dates back 154 years, built in the 1880's as a judge's home complete with stables, servants quarters and an Indian bread oven. The Adobe walls are made from a combination of the red earth mixed with straw and watered mud, the original wooden beams (viga), carved out spaces for ornament patron saints of the Catholic faith and the kiva fireplace is still seen. Our room is called Tesuque after a local Pueblo Indian tribe.

The Pueblo Indians are one of the oldest cultures in the nation, their name is Spanish for "stone masonry village dweller. They built villages, often on top of high mesas or in hollowed-out natural caves at the base of canyons called pueblos north of Santa Fe.

These multiple room structures are made of cut sandstone faced with adobe or the adobe was poured into forms or made into sun-dried bricks to build walls that are often several feet thick. Ladders are used to reach the upper apartments of a pueblo complex. Many Pueblo Indians still live in pueblos that have been occupied by their people for hundreds of years.

We wandered into the town and had a wonderful Mexican meal at The Shed, carefully choosing green chilli on the side as advised by our hotel receptionist. She had carefully instructed us not to drink water or beer if we found our meal too hot but eat the bread as it takes the heat away. Indeed there are chillies everywhere, hanging up as decorations and even our door knocker is a metallic chilli.
