Atlanta, Georgia, United States
Monday, May 2, 2016

As the Highway to Nashville passes right through the centre of Atlanta, and within a mile of the National Park Service's historic site devoted to Martin Luther King, we decided to stop there. It's where he was born, lived, worked, worshipped, and is buried.

The site includes the Ebenezer Baptist Church where Dr. King and his father were Pastors. On the short walk there, we passed through the International World Peace Rose Garden and the "Behold" Monument.

From the site's information:

Martin Luther King, Jr. (January 15, 1929 – April 4, 1968) an American Baptist minister, activist, humanitarian, and leader in the African-American Civil Rights Movement and is best known for his role in the advancement of civil rights using nonviolent civil disobedience based on his Christian beliefs.

A young boy grows up in a time of segregation…A dreamer is moved by destiny into leadership of the modern civil rights movement, he was an instrument for social change.

Dr. King was assassinated on April 4, 1968 at the Lorraine Motel in Memphis, Tennessee and was brought home to be buried in the Sweet Auburn community. On April 9, 1968, his funeral took place at Historic Ebenezer Baptist Church and Morehouse College.

The International World Peace Rose Gardens program is a worldwide effort to help youth recognize the importance and value of peace. In 1992, the Martin Luther King, Jr. "I Have a Dream" World Peace Rose Garden was planted here and it is an artistic interpretation of Dr. King’s life and ideals of peace through nonviolence. The garden’s starburst design reflects Dr. King’s ideals using the Official Flower of the United States, the rose.

----

The visitor centre had some powerful clips from the days of segregation including chilling rhetoric of those who back then hated blacks and treated them as a sub class. Most of the visitors were black, I wondered how they must now feel about the history.

Dr. King's speeches were played over speakers at many points on the site, and being in the place he was brought up, hearing his voice, and thinking about the history was moving.

Negotiating the in places 8 lane highway in Atlanta was something we survived rather than enjoyed. The local drivers are expert lane weavers and give no warnings. All is fine until you yourself need to get across three lanes to take the correct road splitting off - while others are moving into the space you'd just managed to work out you'd move into. We worked as a team, back seat driving was a necessity "no-ones coming to your left now, get over", "you need to work two lanes to your right", and so on. No problem, and no wrong turns! We swapped drivers at the Visitors center, so we both had a turn, and both got a bit sweaty! We also experienced the fastest changing traffic lights we've seen - they went green for about 4 seconds, so just one car moves forward as a pulse to join the main highway. The red lasted a similar time. It seemed to work.

Atlanta has the worlds busiest airport and plenty of planes were seen overhead as we drove through.

We were pleased to get to the other side of Atlanta, where the driving reverted to its normal very easy style, and stopped about 60 miles on at a completely random place just to break the journey towards Country Music Land.
