Houston, Texas, United States
Thursday, May 12, 2016

Leaving the antebellum mansions and swamps behind we drove west today through Cajun and Creole country towards Houston.

West coastal Louisiana is dotted with smaller communities that are cut off by bayous, coastal marshes and inland swamps. The erosion of the wetlands threatens whole communities and the silting up of the river is affecting fishing and shrimping in the Gulf. These coastal communities were devastated by Hurricane Katrina. We drove on elevated roads for miles with deciduous trees growing either side in the swamps. Reminding ourselves of the 2 million alligators living in the area made concentrated driving especially as the bridge sides are very low!! Not sure if you had an accident and went over of your chances.

The annual Crawfish festival was held last weekend where Crawfish races are one of the main attractions, together with dancing the Cajun waltz and two step to traditional Cajun and Zydeco music. Contestants can also compete to eat the most crawfish by weight in 45 minutes, a great missed opportunity!

French used to be spoken here as the main language but Roosevelt decided all children should speak English which nearly wiped out the language. However the older residents fought back and mainly through Cajun music the traditions have been kept alive. The lady in the TOURIST INFORMATION CENTRE at Breaux Bridge told us exactly the same thing and she tries hard to keep this tradition going as she is passionate about her French ancestry.

We passed by Lafayette, 135 miles east of New Orleans, and started to notice the beginning of the oil refineries, long before the Texas state border.

We had no desire to fight the traffic in Houston so bypassed it to its south. On this approach we had a torrential storm and visibility was horrendous on the four lane interstate. Luckily Alan was driving but even he found it hairy. Houston is the fourth biggest city in USA and is renowned for its traffic problems so best avoided.

Alan is delighted to discover the following, but don't worry I will now be driving much more to 'allow Alan to rest' and there's no way I'm keeping up with the traffic!

'SPEED LIMIT 85. Texas Speed Limit - Rural Freeways

The maximum speed limit on rural freeways and interstates in Texas is 85 miles per hour. "Rural freeways" are the sections of major highways that passing through rural and sparsely populated areas, and can safely allow faster driving.
Radar technology is not exact, and as a general rule an officer will not pull you over for exceeding the speed limit by less than 5 mph (90 mph on a rural freeway.'

We stopped at our first Walmart for supplies. I can honestly say I've never been in such a large single storey shop. I only went in a quarter of it as well. The size of the containers and packets is incredible, jaw dropping, with everything from cheese to yoghurt to meat to soft drinks. It does reflect the size of other observations we make constantly too......

Alan has just spotted a mocking bird in the palm tree outside our window!! Christina I need your help!
