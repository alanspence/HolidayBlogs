Lubbock, Texas, United States
Sunday, May 15, 2016

Today is our longest drive, just under six hours, 385 miles. I hear you all groan but it's not like the UK roads. We are loving it. Speed limit is 75 or 80 and the roads are empty, junctions just flow onto the next one and we are happy seeing the changing scenery, chatting and listening to our audio book.

USA judges the speed you are allowed to drive at perfectly. So you are driving along, speed limit 75. All of a sudden it changes to 60 for no apparent reason. Before you can blink there's a railway track, or a smaller road crossing or you are approaching a hamlet. As soon as you pass, for example the town, the speed increases quickly to 35, 45, 55, 60 and before you know it's back to 75. Roadworks reduce to 50-60, no 40 mph cordoned off lanes to be seen!

Leaving behind San Antonio we drove through Leon Valley, our thoughts turning momentarily homeward before settling into the drive. We have constant What's App messages with the kids and partners/son-in-law and USA family we are heading towards as well as lots of Face Timing so we feel in touch. We are definitely being tracked.

The Interstate quickly became only two lanes, the traffic thinned and driving was easy. The countryside is constantly changing and a contrast to Louisiana of course. The endless tall forests have been left behind. Scrub land, lower trees dot the red soil and cacti by the roadside. We went over a high mountain range with views for miles and very few houses or industry. For this stretch of the journey we are making sure the tank is full as petrol stations are less frequent but then at $25 (£17) to get from quarter full to a full tank of petrol we aren't complaining.

Although the main oil drilling sites in Texas are to the west of our route we saw many small drilling hammers, pump jacks or hammer heads, and natural gas drilling from the road. Texas is still home to 45 percent of the nation’s operating rigs although production is dropping and rigs closing.

One of the most surprising thing on the journey was seeing wind farms stretching for an incredible distance. Looking up how many, and taking care not to use Wikipedia as I have been accused of copying and pasting from (not guilty!!), we found there were 1300 wind turbines supplying 10% of Texas' electricity. Farmers lease their land to the wind producers giving them another source of income. The wind industry of course also creates jobs and from some of the run down roadside buildings and industry this is a welcome addition.
