New Orleans, Louisiana, United States
Monday, May 9, 2016

Breakfast was served on the verandah with the wonderful view from the bluff to the Mississippi. People walking and running on the path that runs along the bluff edge all looked over to us and with a smile said "Hi, how's it going", or similar - everyone here is friendly and rather than eye contact being avoided, everyone acts like a friend.

The owner told us the rivers speed is 9-11 knots, an interesting consideration for the sailors. For the non sailors amongst us that's too strong to sail Flyer upstream so sailing takes place on the nearby Oxbow Lakes. MJ will be pleased to know my A Level geography knowledge had already allowed me to inform Alan about their formation.

We drove south on a two lane road via Baton Rouge, stopping at Starbucks for a coffee and quick chat to Michelle. Hope William is feeling better soon. We passed several massive oil and petrochemical plants which had initially saved the economy of New Orleans but since the 1950's slump and 1980's oil crash the city has not fared well. Of course it was in this area that the BP oil spill in the Gulf of Mexico occurred.

The approach to New Orleans is on a elevated road above the swampy Lake Pontchartrain which seemed to go on for miles.

Talking to a friendly local at the tram stop reminded of us of Hurricane Katrina in 2005. He told us about how he sat on his roof for two days when the floods hit, his dog drowned after it fell off the roof, and he watched a dead woman float by, the emotional scars still clearly present. The coastline of New Orleans was devastated when the levees breached and 80% of the city flooded. Some areas just off the main tourist areas are still struggling to rebuild. The stark divisions between rich and poor are quickly seen, also white and black. Of course there were also the 2009 hurricanes which caused damage in catastrophic proportions. The law suit against the Government Department is ongoing as they are accused of being guilty of negligence in the building of the levees when human lives were lost and many properties destroyed.

The streetcar has mahogany seats, brass fittings and exposed ceiling light bulbs and has rumbled along for more than 150 years, 24 hours a day, symbolising the charm and romance of the City of New Orleans. Taking it to the river front and French Quarter gave us an introduction to the vibrant and colourful city of jazz. The city began as a French Canadian outpost in 1718 at the mouth of the Mississippi River, the buildings reflecting a mix of Spanish and French influence.

Dinner at Deja Vu had both Cajun and Creole dishes. Alan chose to have a starter called Gumbo, a fish and rice stew commonly seen in many resturants which uses fish caught in the Gulf. Cajuns trace their ancestry to the Arcadians, colonists from rural France and are the largest French speaking minority in USA. Creoles tend to have urban connections to NO. Then we both had po-boys which are meals contained in long bread. The Deja Vu is open 24/7 - the waitress said it gets dangerous after dark though - just the excuse our generation needs not to stay out late!

The very friendly southern style continued on the trolley ride back to our hotel with conversations between strangers all around us - we got some great tips on New Orleans.
