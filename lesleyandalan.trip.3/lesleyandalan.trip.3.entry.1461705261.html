Cherokee, North Carolina, United States
Wednesday, April 27, 2016

Staying a second night in Asheville. Today visited Clingmans Dome in the 500 million year old Smoky Mountains, USA's most visited National Park and part of the Indian Reservation. Stopped first at the Oconaluftee Visitor Centre before a steep climb to 6200' to the highest point of the park where the slopes are covered with spruce-fir forests on the peaks. Moved on to the Museum of the Cherokee Indian.

Walkway to highest point at Clingmans Dome
Water carried through plants is released through leaves as a vapour. The vapour from the conifers in the Smoky Mountains contains terpenes, an organic chemical which when released gives the smoke-like haze which gives the mountains their name.The acid fallout damages the parks soil, streams, plants and animals. Unfortunately many of the Hemlock trees which once dominated the parks moist soil have been infested by non native insects which have killed many trees and disrupted the habitat of the songbirds, squirrels and trout. The release of a predator beetle that feeds only on this adelgid aims to save the trees remaining. Hemlock bark used to be used for tanning leather.

Today's Cherokee
The guide, a Eastern Band Cherokee lives on the reservation next to the National Park on a 'windy back road'. He gave an interesting account of how the East band of Cherokee Indians have their own laws, jail, schools etc which are all recognised by the Government. In 1819 the Cherokee signed a treaty and had to give up all their land in the Smokies. Sixty families received land grants and gained citizenship, Oconaluftee Cherokee.
