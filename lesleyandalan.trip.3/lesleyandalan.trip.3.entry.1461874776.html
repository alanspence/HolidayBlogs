Charleston, South Carolina, United States
Friday, April 29, 2016

Charleston used to be an important rice growing and trading colony with influences from the West Indies and Africa. It was a key port and trade centre for the slave industry with slave auction houses clustered near the Copper River. 40% of all slaves entered North America through Charleston.

The first shots of the civil war were fired from the harbour area. After the war the labour intensive rice plantations became uneconomical without slave labour so Charleston declined in importance.

Its scary to think that there was a mass shorting in 2015 at the black Methodist church in Charleston and a reminder of the racially fraught past and its effect on the present.

Today the temperature is in the high 80's, a contrast to the cooler London and Kent although Stephen and Jo are having good weather in Cornwall.

Walked through the market areas selling sweet grass and bamboo baskets and then wandered in cobblestone streets and Rainbow Row in the French Quarter to see the antebellum mansions. The cobblestone roads were originally built from stones from the English ships which arrived in the 1670s and needed to offload their ballast to take the locally grown golden rice back to England.

The local people are extremely friendly and interested to know more about us especially when they realise we are from the UK. Several art gallery owners assumed we were from the cruise ship as we were English. One had been to Oxford to study Winston Churchill and was amazed our daughter and son in law lived near Chartwell where she had visited.

Decided to take a walking tour to learn about the history of the city and take us to hidden sites we would not have discovered. The aroma of jasmine and white lilac is everywhere from the house gardens and street displays.

We ended the day with an excellent seafood dinner at Shem Creek in Mount Pleasant watching pelicans, dolphins and all the river traffic
