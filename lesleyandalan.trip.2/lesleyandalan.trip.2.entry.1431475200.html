Hong Kong, Guangxi Zhuang, China
Wednesday, May 13, 2015

Our hotel is situated in Kowloon, mainland HK. Today we went on a tour to Hong King Island through the Cross Harbour Tunnel. Drivers here are so different to China. No one cuts you up, they keep to their lanes, practically no scooters or electric bikes are seen. Such a different experience. As the guide said to us you suddenly feel safe. The temperature is 30 degrees with 77% humidity although rain looks very possible later.

We passed the Happy Valley Horse racing track which occupies the largest stretch of flat land in HK Island. It was originally a swamp but has been used for racing since 1846. Wednesday nights are the most important race night and can host races for 55,000 people. The guide explained that betting was a big occupation for these locals and is the only legal form of gambling.

We visited Stanley Market on the South side of the Island. Stanley was originally a sleepy fishing village before the British moved in. The market offers reasonable priced clothes and accessories which apparently the tourists and locals both use.

Victoria Peak on the Island offers spectacular views when clear. Exclusive residences for the super rich families litter the hillsides, the maids and nannies were seen collecting the kids from pre-school. Kidnapping is a problem in the area.

The Victoria Peak Tram, named by the British after Queen Victoria, takes tourists by a single steel cable funicular railway up the steep wooded hillsides to admire the view. Torrents of water down the hillside can disrupt the trams operation during the rainy summer season. 90% humidity is reached in summer and air conditioning is used everywhere including the London style double decker buses.

Norman Fosters HSBC designed building could be seen from the top. It cost HK $5.302 Bn, making it the most expensive building when it opened in 1985. Since then many new buildings have and continue to emerge. The building next to our hotel used to be the tallest residential building but now downtown another one has been built with an additional floor so it can claim the award. Norman Foster also designed the new airport in 1998.

We then continued to Aberdeen Harbour where the British landed in 1841. It is filled with wooden fishing boats and multi million pound boats alongside each other. Over fishing and pollution have decimated the fishing industry.

The floating restaurants are beautifully decorated. Jumbo is a 2000 people floating restaurant but now sits on the sea floor but is said to have served 30 million customers.

The bay is no longer sheltered from the typhoon by the mountains due to the sea reclamation but people still live totally on the fishing boats which have air conditioning, washing machines, Tv's from their generators. The boats waste still goes into the harbour which is why they don't drink the water. The tour sampans are driven by the women as the men are out fishing.

All kids learn English in school although some have very little opportunity to practice their skills.

Having dogs and cats in Hong Kong is becoming fashionable for the rich to have.

No heavy industry is coated in HK due to the mountainous landscape. The people are hard working often for six days a week/overtime to raise funds for healthcare as there is no welfare service. If you don't work, you don't eat or get health care. Factory workers live near their employment to keep costs down in government accommodation.
In 2014 the unemployment rates as 3.4%. The number one industry in HK is textiles, jewellery is seventh. In 2008 many people were laid off and now people have to work harder for less money.

Lots of construction can be seen using bamboo scaffolding. Unlike China the buildings are being completed in HK. Our guide was obsessed by money and the wealth of the residents. She felt very strongly about the recent student protests and the pending elections in 2017. Again, like our China tour guides they expressed their opinions very clearly.

Went to seafood restaurant by river in the evening.,last night with our fellow travellers as we have extended our trip by one day.
